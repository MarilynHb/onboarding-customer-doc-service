Customer Service · Step 2

The Domain layer

~3 days Plain classes EMI entities

You have an empty solution. Now you fill in the Domain layer: the classes and enums that describe what a customer is at Moneygate. No persistence, no APIs, no validators yet. Just the shape of the data.

Keep these as plain data classes for now. Public setters, simple properties, annotations on the fields. We will revisit encapsulation and behavior later when the Service layer comes in. The point of this step is to get the shape right, not to make the classes clever.

What you'll build

Inside CustomerService.Domain, three folders.

Domain/Common

  • BaseEntity (abstract). Properties: Id (Guid), CreatedAtUtc (DateTime), ModifiedAtUtc (DateTime?). Every entity inherits from this.

Domain/Enums

  • CustomerType: Individual, Corporate
  • IdentificationType: NationalId, Passport, DrivingLicense, TaxIdentificationNumber, CompanyRegistrationNumber
  • OnboardingApplicationStatus: Draft, Submitted, InReview, Approved, Rejected, Cancelled
  • CustomerStatus (optional): Pending, Active, Inactive

Domain/Entities

  • OnboardingApplication - The form where customers submit their onboarding information
  • Customer - The main entity representing a customer. One application per customer.
  • IndividualProfile - The profile for individual customers.
  • CorporateProfile - The profile for corporate customers.
  • CustomerIdentification - Information used to identify a customer. Later this will be linked to documents.

Use annotations on the fields: [Required], [MaxLength], [Key], [EmailAddress], [Phone] where they apply. These come from System.ComponentModel.DataAnnotations.

What you'll learn

  • How to model an EMI customer: the split between a Customer and its profile, why identifications are separate, where the application sits
  • Inheritance with an abstract BaseEntity
  • Navigation properties (forward and inverse), even though no database exists yet
  • When to flatten and when to split: address and contact info live on Customer because they have no independent lifecycle. Profile and identifications are separate because they do.
◆
C# depth moment: DateOnly vs DateTime. A date of birth is a date. A "created at" timestamp is a moment in time. C# 10 added DateOnly so we don't have to pretend a birthday is midnight UTC. Use DateOnly for DateOfBirth, IncorporationDate, IssueDate, ExpiryDate. Use DateTime with Utc in the name for the timestamps.
⌥
Design note: why no separate Address or Contact entity. We considered them and merged them in. An address and a contact have no life of their own outside the customer they belong to: they are not shared, they are not queried independently. A separate table would add joins without buying anything. When something has no independent lifecycle, it lives on its parent.
