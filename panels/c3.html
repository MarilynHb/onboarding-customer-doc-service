Application contracts

~3-4 days Interfaces + DTOs Validation

The Application layer is where you describe what the service needs, without saying how. You define the interface the data layer must implement, the DTOs that cross the boundary, and the validation rules. No database code, no business logic yet. Just contracts.

What you'll build

In CustomerService.Application:

  • An ICustomerStore interface: GetById, GetAll, Add, Update, Delete. All async, all taking a CancellationToken. This is the contract the Store layer will fulfill later.
  • DTOs for the boundary: CreateCustomerRequest, UpdateCustomerRequest, CustomerResponse. These are not your Customer entity. They're shaped for the outside world.
  • FluentValidation validators: CreateCustomerRequestValidator (email looks like an email, names not empty, date of birth in the past, and so on).

What you'll learn

  • Why interfaces are defined in Application but implemented elsewhere (dependency inversion)
  • Why DTOs are separate from entities, and what breaks if you expose entities directly
  • Validation with FluentValidation, and why it lives here rather than in controllers
  • How a layer can be fully written and reviewed before any implementation exists

Suggested approach

  1. Define ICustomerStore. Think carefully about the method signatures. What does each return? What does GetById return when nothing is found?
  2. Write the three DTOs. Notice CreateCustomerRequest has no Id (the system assigns it) and no status (it always starts Registered). CustomerResponse includes both. This difference is the whole point of having DTOs.
  3. Add the FluentValidation NuGet package to the Application project.
  4. Write CreateCustomerRequestValidator. Rules: first and last name required and under some length, email valid, date of birth in the past and the person at least 18.
  5. You can't run anything yet, but you can write unit tests for your validators. A validator with no implementation behind it is still fully testable. Write a couple.
⌥
Why DTOs feel like duplication at first. It looks redundant to have a Customer entity and a CustomerResponse that hold similar fields. The payoff comes later: your entity can change its internals freely without breaking your API, and you never accidentally expose a field (like an internal flag) that the outside world shouldn't see. Write a note about one field that should exist on the entity but never on the response.

C# depth moment: records for DTOs. DTOs are a perfect use for record types: immutable, value-based equality, concise syntax. Read about records and make your DTOs records.
